Berlin

Berlin is the capital and largest city of Germany, with a population of approximately 3.7 million people within the city limits and over 6 million in the greater metropolitan area. The city lies in northeastern Germany at the confluence of the Spree and Havel rivers. It serves as a major European hub for politics, culture, media, and science.

History

The city traces its origins to the 13th century, when two small fishing settlements, Cölln and Berlin, merged on opposite banks of the Spree. It later became the seat of the Hohenzollern dynasty and, by the 19th century, the capital of the Kingdom of Prussia. After German unification in 1871, it grew into one of the largest industrial cities in Europe. The German capital suffered enormous destruction during the Second World War; by 1945, approximately 40 percent of its buildings had been damaged or destroyed. Following the war, the city was divided among the Allied powers, and the construction of the Berlin Wall in 1961 physically separated it into an eastern and a western half for nearly three decades. The wall fell on November 9, 1989, reuniting the city and setting the stage for German reunification the following year. Since then, it has undergone dramatic urban renewal and has re-established itself as Germany's political center.

Geography and Climate

The city covers an area of approximately 892 square kilometers, making it nine times larger than Paris by land area. It sits on a flat glacial plain at an elevation of around 34 meters above sea level. Forests, lakes, and rivers cover roughly one-third of its territory, giving it an unusually green character for a major European capital. The Tiergarten, a vast park in the city center, was once a royal hunting ground and now serves as the lungs of the city. Berlin has a temperate oceanic climate, with mild summers and cold winters. Snowfall occurs regularly between December and February, while July is typically the warmest month, with average highs around 24 degrees Celsius.

Culture and Arts

The city is home to three UNESCO World Heritage Sites, over 170 museums, and more than 300 galleries. It hosts the Berlin International Film Festival, known as the Berlinale, one of the most prestigious film festivals in the world. The city's music scene ranges from the Berlin Philharmonic, considered one of the finest orchestras globally, to a thriving electronic music culture centered around nightclubs that have become internationally famous. Street art is woven into the urban fabric of the city; the East Side Gallery, a preserved 1.3-kilometer section of the former wall, displays murals by artists from around the world. Berlin's culinary scene reflects its history as a crossroads of cultures, blending German traditions with Turkish, Vietnamese, and Middle Eastern influences introduced by successive waves of immigrants.

Economy

Berlin is Germany's largest city economy and home to numerous startup companies, technology firms, and creative industries. It attracts more venture capital than any other German city and has earned the nickname "Silicon Allee." Major employers include Siemens, Deutsche Bahn, and Zalando, alongside a rapidly growing biotech sector. Tourism also plays a significant role; over 14 million visitors arrive annually, drawn by the city's history, nightlife, and cultural offerings. The city government has invested heavily in public transportation, expanding its network of trains, trams, and buses to connect all districts efficiently.
